Identity and Values

Explain a situation where you have made an ethical decision. Discuss how you weighed up the values involved in that decision, the decision you made, and how you reflect on the decision now.
When I was around 18 years old in my hometown, I was walking into the city centre to meet a few friends when I heard a commotion coming from the bus station. As I arrived I saw a man who was clearly in his 20's assaulting a young boy and his girlfriend who were both 13 years old. Obviously this is a terrifying situation to walk into, but I decided to run over and stop it before it got any more serious. I managed to get the attackers attention, and I began being assaulted instead of the young couple. I managed to wrestle control of him and contained him there whilst being punched and spat on, until a passing taxi phoned the police. The police arrived and took me to the station to give a statement. A few weeks later I had to return to the police station and take part in an identity parade. The guy who attacked us was well known, and this event was the one that finally led to him being sentenced to prison time. This was obviously scary as it is a small town and some kind of retaliation by him or his friends wasn't out of the question. I knew it was the right thing to do and I believed it was worth the potential risk, as I saw first hand how dangerous he was. I felt very nervous walking around my town after that, and kept thinking I saw the guy again, but I never did, and eventually I forgot about it. If I remember correctly, he received 18 months in prison, and I left my hometown to move to London shortly after so I have no idea what became of anyone else involved. It's hard to say why I decided to run in, but I imagine it has something to do with having 3 younger siblings, and a desire to protect them maybe. I just sort of did it then the rest was a bit of a blur.

Describe how your culture and the people around you have influenced your values and identity.
Being British and raised there I would like to think I was taught to have good manners, to not always take things too seriously, including yourself, and to love a good queue! But I have also lived in France as a child, Australia, Canada, and Sweden as an adult, and of course my new home in New Zealand. This has given me really valuable insight into how other cultures live, the different values they have, and the values we all share. Without fail they have all been incredibly welcoming to me, and I'd like to think I have returned that kindness. The biggest influence on my values, and the main reason i've been able to do what I have with my life so far is my Dad. I still remember the chats we used to have when I was very young, about treating all people with respect and I think he gave me some good morals to live by. We also moved to the South of France when I was very young which gave me a taste of adventure and the realisation there is a massive world out there, and I want to see as much of it as possible! Perhaps it's because of how much I have moved around, but personally I have never really put too much value into identity, I feel like values are much more important to me, unless i'm misunderstanding the term.

Identify your strengths and how they will support you during your learning journey.
When motivated, I can push really hard to achieve my goals. For example, I decided I wanted to move to Sweden as I loved the food and wanted to shape my career around it. I started sending resumes out and eventually got a call to come and interview. The problem was, I was in France at that time and the interview was the next day, but I said yes and then started trying to figure out how to get there after. I managed to get there in time and got the job. Then moved there with a couple of weeks notice. I really feel like i thrive in these types of situations. I feel like I enjoy the pressure and I always have to be moving towards something.

Evaluate your limitations in terms of your learning and career development. How might these affect your learning journey?
The same thing that makes me enjoy and thrive in situations like the one I outlined above, also makes me get disinterested in things much quicker than average. It's common to move around a lot when you work as a chef, but I definitely moved jobs more than most people. I feel like when I stop feeling the pressure, I begin to get complacent and lose interest, which makes my performance drop below the levels I pride myself on. I know this is definitely going to happen as a developer, and probably on this course, but I am very aware of it and understand I need to address it if I am to be successful.

Share an example of when you were trying to work productively with others, but there was resistance or tension. Discuss strategies you tried at the time, how effective they were, and your reflections on what other strategies you would try now and why.
This is a perfect question as I have had this issue so many times in the kitchen. When I was promoted to a position of leadership recently, I just assumed everybody was going to work hard and pull their weight because that's just what I expected. I am very timid, hate confrontation, and definitely a push over so one of the members of the team started taking full advantage of this by leaving early without asking, and not even doing half of the job expected of them. Instead of speaking to them or my manager about it, I just started taking on the extra work they were missing and doing it myself, whilst fuming on the inside. This went on for a week or so, until I'd almost reached breaking point and had to tell my manager. Because I hadn't addressed it myself earlier, it became harder for me to prove that I was doing extra work and the other member of staff had been taking advantage. This situation went on for a couple of months and sent my mental health on a pretty bad downward trajectory. I stopped going to the gym and looking after my health as much, and I still haven't quite got back to where I want to be with that. This could have all been solved early on if i'd have just spoken up and not let it stew. I know I can't let this type of thing happen again, and i'm sure it won't on this course as everybody seems super driven. The main takeaway from this is to let my feelings be known earlier and don't feel like i'ma burden for raising very valid issues.
