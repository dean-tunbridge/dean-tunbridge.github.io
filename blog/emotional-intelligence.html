Emotional intelligence

What is emotional intelligence?

Emotional intelligence (EQ) is the ability to recognise, understand, manage, and express emotions, as well as understanding the emotions of others. There are several aspects to it including; being self aware, being empathic, having good social skills, and being able to manage and regulate your emotions, impulses, and behaviors.

How does it differ from IQ

Emotional intelligence focuses on emotional and social abilities rather than purely intellectual capacities. IQ measures analytical and logical reasoning and is generally fixed during adulthood, it is also largely genetic which means you can't really change it or improve it. EQ however can be developed and improved through self-awareness, practice, and learning from experiences. Below are some examples from studies that suggest improving your EQ is extremely beneficial to not only your personal life, but also your job and career prospects.

  • In educational settings, EQ has been associated with improved academic performance, especially in areas requiring interpersonal skills, collaboration, and effective communication. Students with higher EQ may exhibit better social adjustment and engagement in learning activities
    (Salovey & Mayer, 1990).

  • EQ plays a crucial role in forming and maintaining healthy relationships. Individuals with higher EQ are better at understanding and responding to the emotions of others, which fosters intimacy, trust, and effective communication in personal and professional relationships
    (Gottman & DeClaire, 1997).

  • Research has indicated that EQ skills, such as empathy, emotional regulation, and social skills, are critical for effective leadership and job performance. Leaders with higher EQ are often better at inspiring and motivating teams, resolving conflicts, and fostering a positive work environment
    (Goleman, 1998).

  • Emotional intelligence has been linked to better mental health outcomes, including lower levels of stress, anxiety, and depression. EQ skills, such as emotional self-awareness and resilience, contribute to overall psychological well-being and adaptive coping strategies
    (Brackett & Mayer, 2003).
  • Studies have shown that EQ can be a stronger predictor of career success than IQ alone. Individuals with higher EQ tend to have better interpersonal relationships, higher levels of job satisfaction, and are more likely to advance in their careers
    (Goleman, 1995; Bradberry & Greaves, 2009).
